    01 · Context

    Why now — and where we stand today.

    Every department runs on dashboards and reports. With multiple systems (POS, SAP ERP & CAR, Foodics, Odoo, SQL Server, Fabric, Power BI), growing integrations and accelerating AI initiatives across Kuwait, KSA and Baqala, the question is no longer whether we have data — it is: can we trust every number we use to run the business?

    Business
    • Different versions of the same KPI
    • Unclear data ownership
    • Undocumented business definitions
    Technology
    • Limited end-to-end lineage
    • Missing metadata & catalog
    • Documentation gaps, difficult impact analysis
    Operations
    • Manual reconciliation between systems
    • Reactive — not preventive — quality checks
    • Rework and duplicated analysis
    Security
    • Ungoverned Excel exports & offline copies
    • Uncontrolled sharing of sensitive data
    • No classification of critical information
    More data creates more opportunity — and more risk. These are not technology issues; they are business risks.
    01 · Context

    A scenario we all recognize.

    This is what the absence of governance costs us — not in theory, in a normal working week.

    Monday 09:00

    Two numbers, one KPI

    An executive dashboard shows yesterday's sales for a region at KWD 412K. The commercial team pulls the same figure from SAP CAR: KWD 405K. Which one goes to management?

    Tuesday – Wednesday

    The reconciliation hunt

    Three teams spend two days tracing the gap — a timing cut-off plus a returns-handling difference. Nobody made an error. There was simply no single governed definition.

    The real cost

    Trust is the casualty

    The number was eventually explained — but from that day, every figure on that dashboard gets double-checked. Reconciling numbers replaces acting on them.

    Every hour spent asking “which number is right?” is an hour not spent growing the business.

    Illustrative scenario — variations of this occur in every multi-system retailer operating without governed KPI definitions.

    01 · Context

    Risks if nothing changes.

    Business risk

    Wrong decisions built on unreliable or conflicting data.

    Financial risk

    Losses from inaccurate reporting and rework. Gartner estimates poor data quality costs organizations an average of USD 12.9M per year.

    Operational risk

    Slow issue resolution and high manual effort across teams.

    Compliance risk

    Regulatory penalties and audit findings from ungoverned data.

    Strategic risk

    AI and analytics built on a weak, untrusted foundation.

    External & non-optional — regulatory drivers

    🇰🇼 Kuwait — CITRA

    Data privacy protection regulations governing how personal data is collected, processed and protected.

    🇸🇦 KSA — PDPL

    The Personal Data Protection Law is in force and enforceable, with financial penalties — directly relevant to our KSA operations.

    💳 PCI-DSS

    Payment-card data flowing through 150+ store POS systems must meet strict handling and security standards.

    Data without governance is a risk multiplier.

    02 · Vision

    Our solution: an enterprise governance framework.

    Eight pillars, supported by policies, standards, processes, tools, people and culture.

    1 · Governance Framework

    Council, roles, RACI, decision rights and escalation paths.

    2 · Data Quality

    Rules, automated scorecards, monitoring and issue resolution.

    3 · Master Data

    Golden records for products, stores, suppliers and customers.

    4 · Data Catalog & Metadata

    Searchable inventory of data assets with business context.

    5 · Lineage & Impact Analysis

    Trace every KPI from POS to executive report.

    6 · Security & Privacy

    Classification, access control, CITRA / PDPL / PCI-DSS alignment.

    7 · Reporting Governance

    Certified reports — one governed version of every executive KPI.

    8 · AI Governance

    Responsible AI, model monitoring, prompt & risk governance.

    Supported by: Policies · Standards · Processes · Tools · People · Culture
